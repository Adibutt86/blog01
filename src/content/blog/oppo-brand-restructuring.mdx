The smartphone industry is currently undergoing a massive transformation, driven not just by a severe memory supply crisis, but also by significant strategic shifts among major players. Oppo has recently confirmed a long-rumored overhaul of its brand portfolio, fundamentally altering the market presence of both OnePlus and Realme across Asian and Western regions.

According to internal sources speaking to Xataka Móvil, this move comes after months of speculation. The Chinese tech giant has opted to withdraw its sub-brands from highly competitive battlegrounds, choosing instead to focus them on regions where they maintain strong sales figures.

This strategic pivot resolves the ongoing uncertainty surrounding the future of OnePlus in Europe. Earlier this year, rumors circulated about OnePlus dismantling its operations outside of China. Despite initial denials from executives, the reality has now unfolded. The outcome is a distinct geographic division: Realme will cease its Chinese operations to focus on its European success, while OnePlus will permanently shut down its presence in both Europe and the United States.

## OnePlus Bids Farewell to the West as Realme Solidifies its Position

The withdrawal of OnePlus from European and US markets represents the end of an era for a brand originally built for tech enthusiasts. The remaining inventory of OnePlus smartphones, tablets, and accessories in these regions will be sold off in the coming weeks, marking the end of any new product launches there.

However, to reassure existing customers, the company has pledged to honor software support and warranty coverage for devices already sold until the end of their lifecycle.

Signs of this retreat have been visible for months. In March, executive resignations fueled rumors of an imminent exit. Just before the official announcement, staff cuts in communication departments and web redirects to Oppo's store made the decision an open secret.

On the flip side, this restructuring brings positive news for Realme fans in Europe and Spain. While Realme is exiting its native Chinese market—where it struggled against the dominance of Huawei and Apple—it will continue its operations as usual in Europe. Here, Realme has successfully established a strong foothold in the mid-range and entry-level segments, serving as Oppo's volume-driving brand.

Oppo's official statement emphasizes this strategy: 
> "To further consolidate resources and enhance the synergy of our global product strategy... The OnePlus product roadmap in China remains unchanged. OnePlus will continue offering premium gaming and performance experiences. OPPO's European business remains stable, with strong momentum in the premium flagship segment. We are committed to supporting OnePlus users in Europe and guaranteeing continuity in both service and product experience."

OnePlus also addressed its community directly: 
> "Although we will no longer launch new products in Europe, our commitment to you remains. Backed by OPPO, current OnePlus devices will continue receiving scheduled software updates and security patches... Global users with eligible OnePlus devices will have the option to voluntarily upgrade to the latest version of ColorOS. This allows us to optimize software development, accelerate update delivery, and better leverage our shared R&D capabilities."

## A New Era for the Mid-Range Market

This restructuring also brings significant software changes. Realme UI will be phased out and merged into Oppo’s ColorOS. This is a logical step, considering both Realme UI and OxygenOS (OnePlus's interface) have been based on Oppo’s core Android development for years. Consequently, compatible Realme devices will receive an optional update to migrate to ColorOS. This unified software identity across BBK Electronics brands will streamline the rollout of updates and security patches.

The market will inevitably adjust to these changes. With OnePlus retreating, Oppo will focus its efforts on the premium and high-end segments with its own flagship devices. Meanwhile, future mid-range and entry-level Realme phones sold in Europe may share hardware components, specifications, and supply chains with OnePlus devices manufactured for the Chinese market.

This drastic reorganization is a response to a challenging market environment marked by rising costs for memory, storage, and displays, which have severely squeezed profit margins for affordable devices. Maintaining three distinct brands competing against each other had become financially unsustainable. By centralizing resources, unifying Android interfaces, and strictly delineating the geographic focus of each brand, Oppo aims to secure its long-term viability in the fiercely competitive smartphone industry.
